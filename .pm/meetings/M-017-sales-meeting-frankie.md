## Summary

**Customer knowledge — what the system should capture when inheriting an account**
- Most useful straight away: any past issues, order recency/frequency, year-on-year trend (much of this is already on the system).
- Gaps to add: prune contacts who've left; a clear **main point of contact** + job title ("who's the big dog"); who holds the relationship; and **venue-specific delivery/access quirks** (e.g. "it's upstairs") — Frankie has booked orders blind to access needs.
- Essential vs nice-to-have: **essential** = any issues + main point of contact; **useful** = job titles/roles.
- Tacit knowledge lost if a salesperson leaves: customer preferences/quirks (a customer who wants white tops on the bars; who emails commission which way; who's difficult and needs careful handling).
- Venue delivery info often isn't linked to the venue database — it could be.

**Account plans & site-visit notes**
- Frankie keeps everything in her account plans + separate tabs (e.g. site-visit notes), but those live in **Google Sheets, off-system**. On-system they'd be shared/visible to the team.
- Idea: split the plan into "what we know about the customer" (static, with a completeness view) and "what we'll do about it" (actions). An **actions-to-take** section with add-to-dashboard buttons (site visit, 6-month check-in) that grey out when done; auto-generate summaries/actions from account meetings.
- Ben's caveat: auto-summarising *in-person* meetings could undermine human connection unless framed carefully (fine on Zoom, trickier face-to-face).

**Account maturity**
- Relationships progress transactional → personal (email tone warms — "hi"→"hey", smiley faces, LinkedIn engagement). Once friendly, you can smooth logistics issues and keep trust.
- Ben's caution: don't incentivise *fake* informality — but the customer's own shift to informal tone is a useful signal to mirror.
- Portfolio is mixed: dormant accounts (order once a year for a decade), re-gifted accounts, and genuinely growing ones. Old accounts feel warm if usage continued, cold/at-risk if ordering quietly tailed off — order history + frequency flag this.

**Ownership & transfers**
- Ownership = "all their orders are mine, my responsibility — like my child": accountable for growth and issues, but gets the credit/commission.
- Ownership is transient (accounts move up as they grow, or get handed down when dormant/time-wasting). When NOT to transfer: mid-order or just before a known annual repeat (unfamiliarity can push a customer elsewhere). Main driver = workload efficiency: shed high-maintenance/low-reward or dead accounts to focus on bigger ones (e.g. Tate going full-time).
- **Austin's point:** transfer triggers should key off **open contracts/quotes**, not just a calendar window (a quote four months out may need attention).
- Ben's idea: quarterly review rhythm with an **overlapping handover quarter** — outgoing + incoming AMs both CC'd. Frankie's Paola / Museum-of-the-Home handover worked exactly this way (soft move, shared commission that month).
- Frankie wants advance warning of incoming accounts so she can research/prep (previous AM, LinkedIn) before taking them on.

**Venues & cash orders**
- Venues are usually "dummy"/referring accounts; the cash customers passing through are a **missed development opportunity** because the AM is focused on keeping the venue happy.
- Frankie can't adequately develop venue cash-customer leads (~7 accounts, 10+ venues) on top of everything — high-value leads (e.g. CodeNode's high-spend tech crowd) get missed. Open question: who develops those.

**Roles & responsibilities** (informed by Ben's chat with Axel / Humanness)
- Hierarchy: sales executive → junior account manager → **senior account manager (a role we don't have yet)**. Up the hierarchy, time shifts from transactional to planning/relationships; senior AMs hold fewer, more valuable accounts.
- Rough time split: sales exec ~10-20% relationship work; junior AM ~30-40%; senior AM mostly relationship/planning on a handful of accounts.
- The April commission-structure change has had a real effect; the goal is a shared understanding that progressing means owning bigger, more valuable things — and that letting go is part of it.

**Visibility & tooling**
- Frankie checks quotes vs contracts but can't systematically review all accounts (too many). She maintains her own **colour-coded Google Sheet** (clock = reached out this quarter, tick = ordered, emojis) because it's easier to see everything in one place than the My Accounts page.
- The **My Accounts page** lacks clear up/down (RAG) indicators for YoY and QoQ revenue, and a portfolio-level tactical view ("what's going well, what do I need to do", which accounts have gone quiet/red).
- This must be made easy on-system, especially for AMs with the most accounts — keeping a spreadsheet current across many accounts is very tricky.

**Knowledge sharing**
- Currently informal — templates passed peer-to-peer (Paola shared commission templates; Frankie keeps a self-emailed template folder). Repeating/data-driven emails should be systematised/automated. Commission + email-template capture already done with Nathan and Joel.

**Portfolio & personal development**
- Portfolio grows with the person — Frankie's book moved from mostly-medium to including large accounts (£30k order, Thorns, Tate) with Nathan's help. Compounds: bigger accounts → more money → recognition → more development.
- The **personal + team commission mix** is valued — the personal "little star" motivates beyond the money and is visible when booking in.

**Sales plays** (Axel's term)
- Not SAP-style heavy documents — for us it's the *reasons and tools* to contact accounts (new-business pitch benefits, revival reasons).
- Frankie's idea: commission/discount agreements expire yearly (many are 5 years old and dormant) — use the yearly expiry as a **forced check-in / reason to re-contact** ("your agreement's running out, shall we update it?"), which also reminds them they get commission. Ben flagged this to write down.

## Product / feature ideas (sales ideas bank — candidates, not commitments)
1. Account "essential info" fields: past issues; main point of contact (+ job title/role); relationship-holder; prune contacts who've left.
2. Venue-specific delivery/access notes, linked to the venue database.
3. Customer-preferences / quirks field (the tacit knowledge lost when someone leaves).
4. Account plan split: "what we know" (static, with a completeness indicator) vs "actions to take".
5. Actions-to-take section with add-to-dashboard buttons (site visit, 6-month check-in) that grey out when done; auto-summaries/actions from account meetings.
6. "They're ours now" switch — mark when an account has effectively chosen us; correlate as a lead indicator vs the lag of later orders. (Frankie's caveat: risk of complacency.)
7. Relationship-maturity signal capture (customer tone/informality) — as a signal, without incentivising fake informality.
8. Ownership/handover workflow: quarterly rhythm, overlapping handover quarter, both AMs CC'd; transfer triggers from open contracts/quotes not just the calendar; advance warning of incoming accounts for prep.
9. Venue cash-customer development — surface/assign the cash customers venues refer, so they're not missed.
10. Senior Account Manager role + role-based time-split model (transactional → relational up the hierarchy).
11. My Accounts upgrade: RAG up/down indicators (YoY + QoQ revenue), portfolio-level tactical view, flag accounts that have gone quiet; a customisable personal tracking view to replace Frankie's spreadsheet.
12. Email/commission template library + automation for repeating emails.
13. Sales plays: a library of reasons/tools to contact accounts (new business + revival), incl. commission-agreement yearly-expiry as a forced check-in trigger.
